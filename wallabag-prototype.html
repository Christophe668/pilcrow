The Library You Carry

A read-it-later list isn't a queue — it's a portrait. What we save, and how rarely we return to it, says more about us than what we read.

JK
Jules Koren
aeon.co/essays/the-library · saved yesterday
A reader's library, photographed in late afternoon light. — illustration

The first time I noticed I had a personal library — not a shelf, but a practice — I was waiting for a train in Helsinki. The departure board kept lying about the platform. I opened my reader, expecting the usual queue of unread tabs. Instead I found something stranger: a slow accretion of pieces I had been saving for nearly a decade. Essays on glaciers. A long defense of birds. The full text of a 2014 magazine column about a man who repaired pianos. Most of it I had never finished. Some of it I had forgotten saving. All of it, taken together, looked like a portrait of someone I half-recognized.

It is fashionable to feel guilty about a backlog. The whole genre of read-it-later apps has been pitched, for fifteen years now, as a productivity affordance: capture now, read later, achieve inbox zero on the open web. The premise has always been faintly anxious. Articles are tasks; tasks accumulate; accumulated tasks are debt; debt must be paid. Pocket once advertised itself, in a piece of copy that has aged badly, as a way to "tame the internet."

"What we save, and how rarely we return to it, says more about us than what we read."

But sit with the queue long enough — say, the ten years it took mine to fill — and a different reading suggests itself. Saved articles do not behave like tasks. They behave like books. A friend's bookshelf, glanced at over a glass of wine, is a quiet form of self-disclosure. So is a saved-article list, if you can bear to look at it.

What I noticed, scrolling backward through mine on the platform in Helsinki, was a kind of loose argument I had been making with myself for a decade. About slowness. About craft. About the relationship between a place and the work it produces. I had not realised I was making the argument; I had only been saving things. The library knew before I did.

"A backlog is a self-portrait, drawn slowly, by accident, with a stranger's hand."

There is, I think, a small useful discipline available here, if you'd like one:

  1. Stop measuring the queue by length. The unread count is a productivity metric pretending to be a literary one. Hide it. Most reader apps will let you. The good ones default to it.
  2. Re-read your saves. Once a season, scroll to the bottom of the list — the oldest pieces — and read three. You will find at least one is no longer interesting. That is fine. Archive it. The other two will be sharper than anything you saved this week, because the version of you who saved them has had years to decide whether they were right.
  3. Trust the fold. If a piece has sat in the queue for two years and you still won't archive it, something in you wants it nearby. Leave it. The library is not the to-do list; it is the room.

The third habit is the one I struggled with longest. There is a quiet shame about a long unread list, and the obvious solution — declare bankruptcy, archive everything, start over — feels like decisive housekeeping. It is not. It is the thing a person does when they confuse their library with their inbox. Real libraries have unread shelves. Real libraries are mostly unread shelves. That is the shape of having a library at all.

The portable version of the same room is, oddly, easier to maintain. It does not gather dust. It can be searched. It travels in a pocket. It costs almost nothing to keep. The only thing you have to give up is the urge to drain it — to confuse the queue with the library, the inbox with the room.

So: a small recommendation. The next time you open your reader, do not start with the most recent article. Scroll all the way to the bottom and look at what your past self thought worth carrying. You will find a stranger there. Then come back to today. The library was the point.
